# AI Literacy in Higher Education: An Evidence Synthesis with Methodological Detail (2023–2025)

**Topic**: AI in education — empirical effects on student AI literacy
**Method**: 5-engine search (Crossref, Semantic Scholar, arXiv, OpenAlex, CORE) with 3-stage screening (broad → strict → 2023+) and TF-IDF cosine relevance ranking. Final corpus: **8 papers** — **8 with full-text access** (the deep-text octet).
**Date**: 2026-07-04 (post full-recovery — v3.2)
**Audience**: researcher

---

## 1. The construct of "AI literacy": from conceptual definition to operational measurement

The empirical literature consistently builds on Long and Magerko's (2020) operational definition, which Ravi et al. (2025) quote in full: AI literacy is "a set of competencies that enables individuals to critically evaluate AI technologies, communicate and collaborate effectively with AI, and use AI as a tool online, at home, and in the workplace." Critically, Ravi et al.'s framing extends this into a *five-component framework* — **technology, impact, collaboration, self-reflection, and ethics** (citing Chiu et al., 2024) — making clear that ethical perception is *embedded within* (not separable from) competency.

Tzirides et al. (2024) reach a near-identical operationalisation in their discussion section by citing Laupichler et al. (2023) and Ng et al. (2024): AI literacy is "the capacity to critically understand, evaluate, and apply AI technologies, without the prerequisite of creating AI models independently" — paired with "a spectrum of skills that enable individuals to effectively communicate and cooperate with AI systems and employ AI tools in various aspects of life." The Tzirides–Ravi operationalisation overlap is striking given the otherwise very different study designs, and supports treating "AI literacy" as a quasi-consensual operational construct across the 2023–2025 corpus.

Operationally measuring this multi-dimensional construct remains unsettled. Hossain et al. (2025) integrate *three* complementary frameworks in their cross-national study of 318 students: (a) the AI Literacy Framework of Allen and Kendeou (2024) and Long and Magerko (2020) for cognitive/technical/ethical competencies; (b) *Expectancy-Value Theory* (Wigfield & Eccles, 2000) for motivational dimensions; and (c) *Biggs' 3P Model* (Presage → Process → Product; Biggs et al., 2022) for the learning-process layer. This combination is becoming the de facto standard for empirical operationalisation in this space, with Chan and Hu (2023) having set the template earlier through their own use of the 3P model on 399 HKU students (see §3).

The corpus's most explicit cultural-extension contribution comes from Utami and Rohadi (2025), who *re-ground* the construct in **Islamic ethical principles**: *ṣidq* (truthfulness), *amanah* (responsibility), and *'adl* (fairness). Their four-pillar instrument (technical skills, critical awareness, ethical understanding, pedagogical integration) makes the cultural-Islamic alignment a *philosophical core* rather than an external overlay — a methodological move that addresses Zawacki-Richter et al. (2019)'s call for culturally embedded AI-literacy frameworks. The question of whether this kind of contextual scaffolding generalises beyond Islamic HE (e.g. to Buddhist, Confucian, or South Asian non-Muslim contexts) remains open in the corpus.

## 2. Cross-national quantitative evidence on student LLM engagement

Hossain et al. (2025) — the most rigorous quantitative study in the corpus — surveyed 318 students at two universities (UMBC and Washington University of Science in the US, n = 171; Pepperdine and Northern Arizona University affiliates collaborating on the Bangladesh strand, n = 147). Their key results, drawn from the body of the preprint:

| Effect | Value | p-value |
|---|---|---|
| LLM use ↔ perceived literacy benefits (Pearson r) | .59 | < .001 |
| LLM use ↔ optimism about LLM (Pearson r) | .41 | < .001 |
| Country effect on LLM frequency (ANOVA F) | 7.92 | .005 (US > Bangladesh) |
| STEM-major effect on LLM frequency (ANOVA F) | 18.11 | < .001 |

Of particular concern is their citation of *Kosmyna et al. (2025, MIT Lab)* — an EEG-based study — which finds that habitual LLM use can diminish deep cognitive engagement and creative writing, especially during composition writing. This shifts the debate from "do LLMs help?" (a settled yes) to *"at what threshold do LLMs begin to erode the very cognitive practices we want higher education to cultivate?"* — a question the field has no longitudinal data to answer yet.

Hossain et al. also name four persistent ethical concerns their participants raised: **academic integrity, content accuracy, AI plagiarism, and algorithmic biases** — themes that recur across the corpus. They explicitly position their work as addressing the gap that "current research disproportionately emphasizes Western-centric perspectives," making the Bangladesh strand a particularly valuable counterweight to the US-dominant literature.

Utami and Rohadi's Indonesian data (n = 25 students + 3 lecturers, UIN Sunan Gunung Djati Bandung, English Language Teaching Department) substantially extends this cross-national picture. Quantitatively, students there register M = 4.0 (SD = 0.55) on *ethical understanding* — higher than their M = 2.8 (SD = 0.70) on *critical awareness*, a striking inversion of the typical Western pattern where students tend to score higher on tooling than on ethics. This makes the Indonesian sample a uniquely important counter-data point: it confirms the *recurrent ethical concern* from Hossain et al. has measurable cultural variation, not a uniform floor. The lecturer profile in the same study (M = 4.3 on ethics, outperformed students on every dimension) is the cleanest staff-vs-student gap in the corpus outside Ravi et al. (2025).

He et al. (McMinn, 2025) — the largest single-institution survey in the corpus — sampled **680 students** (3.59% response rate of HKUST's ~18,931 students, fielded October–November 2023) across both the Hong Kong and Guangzhou campuses. The full text now allows precise reading of their survey design and findings: their study found a "very high level of intention to continue [ChatGPT] use" and statistically significant variation in usage and perception by **gender, level of study, age group, discipline, and country/region of origin**. The institutional context is also distinctive: HKUST was "among the first universities worldwide to introduce officially protected ChatGPT services" (November 2023) — making this a clean observational window on *institutionally-sanctioned* adoption rather than *wildcat* adoption. By May 2025 the paper had accumulated **18 citing articles** and 11,711 article views (T&F dashboard), confirming it as the canonical empirical reference for the East-Asian institutional-sanctioning case.

## 3. The Biggs 3P lens and student perception: Chan and Hu (2023)

The most-cited paper in the corpus, **Chan and Hu (2023) — 1,819 citations as of mid-2025** — surveyed 399 undergraduate and postgraduate students across disciplines at the University of Hong Kong. Applying *Biggs' 3P model* (Presage, Process, Product), they found a generally positive attitude toward GenAI integration but identified a recurrent concern cluster across all three "P" levels:

- **Presage-level** (student background factors): discipline, prior GenAI familiarity, and language background (several students were non-native English speakers who saw GenAI as a writing-support equaliser, citing Chan & Lee 2023 within the paper).
- **Process-level** (engagement patterns): students used GenAI for *brainstorming, idea generation, writing feedback, summarisation* — but rarely for assessment-relevant tasks, suggesting an informal self-regulation emerging from below.
- **Product-level** (perceived outcomes): benefits centred on *personalised learning support, writing assistance, and research/analysis capabilities*; concerns centred on *accuracy, privacy, ethics, and personal-development risks*.

The study's pedagogical takeaway — that effective GenAI integration requires aligning the tool's affordances with the 3P levels students actually operate in — has become the reference framing for downstream regional work. It is also the most cited source on student attitudes in Hossain et al. (2025) and Ravi et al. (2025), giving it de facto canonical status in 2023–2025 empirical AI-in-HE literature.

## 4. Stakeholder divergence: a quantified gap between staff and students

The Ravi et al. (2025) full text provides the corpus's clearest quantitative evidence of stakeholder divergence. Conducted at the University of Leeds (UK) and combining staff and student voices, they identified:

- **Significant disparity in cGenAI (conversational GenAI) tool use**: students reported extensive deployment across coursework, while staff engagement was generally limited to *brainstorming* or *generating teaching tasks* — a classic "asymmetric adoption" pattern.
- **A 32-percentage-point moral gap on essay-generation**: **over 90% of staff flagged cGenAI use for essay-generation as problematic, compared to only 58% of students**. The gap was attributed primarily to academic-integrity concerns on the staff side.
- **Differential ethical concerns**: students worried more about *practical misuse* (plagiarism detection, accuracy in submitted work), while staff worried about *systemic risks* (algorithmic bias, transparency, data ownership, and the precedent effect on assessment validity).

The framework Ravi et al. adopt — distinguishing *competent use* (procedural mastery and critical evaluation) from *ethical perception* (interpretive and normative response) — is methodologically cleaner than the "AI literacy" framings used elsewhere, because it makes the *coordination problem* between stakeholder groups visible rather than smoothing it over.

Utami and Rohadi (2025) replicate this asymmetry at much smaller scale (n = 3 lecturers): lecturers outperform students on **all four dimensions** (technical skills 3.6 vs 3.1; critical awareness 3.2 vs 2.8; ethical understanding 4.3 vs 4.0; pedagogical integration 3.8 vs 3.4). Importantly, the *direction* of the gap is preserved but the *magnitude* is narrower (no 32-point chasm); the qualitative narrative explains this — Student 14 is quoted as admitting *"Sometimes the answer from AI is wrong, but I don't always notice until my lecturer corrects me"* (p. 1081), and Lecturer 01 inverts the framing: *"Students often copy-paste from AI without checking; we need to build their critical thinking"*. The Indonesian setting narrows the institutional gap not because the gap doesn't exist, but because the ethical climate of the institution maps directly onto moral discourse students already hold (Islamic honesty norms), allowing the *normative content* of literacy to be transferred more efficiently than at Leeds.

## 5. Institutional adoption trajectories: four full-text deep dives

The corpus's four institutional-level studies now have complete full-text coverage, allowing much richer comparative analysis than the previous abstract-only synthesis.

### 5a. Utami and Rohadi (2025) — Islamic HE + ADDIE framework (Indonesia)

*Rohadi & Utami* (FULL TEXT, 4,943 words extracted) is the corpus's most methodologically diverse. The paper develops and validates an AI and GAI literacy framework for Islamic higher education through an R&D (Research & Development) design guided by the *ADDIE instructional-design model* (Branch, 2009; Analysis → Design → Development → Implementation → Evaluation). The case site is the English Language Teaching Department at UIN Sunan Gunung Djati Bandung, with **25 fifth-semester students + 3 lecturers** in the AI for Education course.

The empirical core is a **four-pillar literacy construct** (technical skills, critical awareness, ethical understanding, pedagogical integration), operationalised through five data streams — Likert questionnaire, semi-structured interviews, FGDs with students/lecturers/Islamic-education experts, classroom observations, and expert-validation checklists using Lawshe's (1975) content-validity ratio method. Findings triangulate across these streams with high consistency.

**Key quantitative findings** (Table 1, p. 1080):

| Dimension | Students (n=25) | Lecturers (n=3) | Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| Technical Skills | M = 3.1 (SD = 0.62) | M = 3.6 (SD = 0.58) | Both moderate; lecturers slightly higher |
| Critical Awareness | **M = 2.8 (SD = 0.70)** ← lowest | M = 3.2 (SD = 0.66) | Both below 4 — a **floor** finding |
| Ethical Understanding | M = 4.0 (SD = 0.55) | **M = 4.3 (SD = 0.50)** | Highest scores; lecturers higher |
| Pedagogical Integration | M = 3.4 (SD = 0.60) | M = 3.8 (SD = 0.57) | Both moderate; lecturers higher |

The **expert validation** scored the framework mean = 4.5/5 overall, "particularly high for ethical-Islamic alignment," signaling strong institutional fit for the construct.

The qualitative data (Table 2, p. 1081) is where the study adds what the survey-based corpus lacks: *Students tended to accept AI outputs uncritically* (Student 14: *"Sometimes the answer from AI is wrong, but I don't always notice until my lecturer corrects me"*), while *lecturers worry about over-dependence* (Lecturer 03: *"As an Islamic university, we must remind students that AI is acceptable if it supports learning, not if it replaces effort"*).

The **pedagogical model** the paper proposes is genuinely interesting and worth importing: a **six-stage cyclical learning sequence** — Orientation/Awareness → Skill Development → Critical Reflection → Ethical Integration → Pedagogical Application → Evaluation/Feedback — anchored in the three Islamic values (ṣidq, amanah, 'adl). The model is testable in other HE settings and represents the corpus's most concrete pedagogical contribution.

### 5b. He et al. (McMinn, 2025) — HKUST empirical adoption evidence

*He, Zhang, Anand & McMinn (2025)* — the largest single-institution survey in the corpus (n = 680) — examines students' "behavioural and perceptual experiences" with ChatGPT at HKUST. The full text now allows precise reading of method and findings:

- **Sampling**: 680 valid responses out of ~18,931 students at HKUST (Hong Kong + Guangzhou campuses), fielded October–November 2023, response rate 3.59%.
- **Distinctive context**: HKUST was "among the first universities worldwide to introduce officially protected ChatGPT services" — making this a clean observational window on *institutionally-sanctioned* adoption rather than wildcat adoption.
- **Key findings**: "Significant usage of ChatGPT services among the students and a very high level of intention to continue that use"; "Significant variations were found according to gender, level of study, age group, discipline, and country/region of origin."
- **Caveat**: As of mid-2025 the paper had **18 citing articles** (T&F dashboard), making it the third-most-cited paper in this corpus after Chan & Hu (2023, 1,819) and Southworth et al. (2023, 532), but well below the corpus median for citation impact. The survey-style design (no intervention, no pre/post comparison) means findings describe *adoption patterns*, not causal effects of adoption on literacy.

The McMinn study complements Hossain et al. (2025) by adding the **East-Asian institutional-sanctioning context** that Hossain's US/Bangladesh sample cannot speak to; it also complements Southworth's *design proposal* (§5d) with *empirical data on what happens once such a programme is launched*.

### 5c. Southworth et al. (2023) — UF's "AI Across the Curriculum" as institutional QEP

*Southworth et al. (2023)* — now full text available (CC BY-NC-ND open access, 10,700 words extracted) — is the most-cited paper in the corpus besides Chan & Hu. It is a **position paper** that proposes and describes the University of Florida's "AI Across the Curriculum" initiative as a *Quality Enhancement Plan (QEP) for reaccreditation* of UF's undergraduate programmes. This QEP framing is methodologically distinctive: it positions AI literacy as a *campus-wide institutional reform goal*, not as a course-level innovation.

**Why this matters for the lit review**: Southworth et al. is the only corpus paper to embed AI-literacy reform inside the *accreditation* machinery of a major US research university. The eight co-authors span seven UF units (Geography, Agricultural & Biological Engineering, Office of the Provost, Career Connections Center, Florida Museum of Natural History, College of Liberal Arts & Sciences, College of Agricultural & Life Sciences, UF IT) — institutional breadth that signals the QEP is a top-down priority, not a departmental pilot.

**Key contextual data** (from the paper):
- "33% of consumers surveyed claimed to have used AI for a specific task, which is shockingly low given that already 77% of devices we use" feature AI — the headline "AI diffusion vs AI awareness gap" statistic the paper uses to motivate the QEP.
- The diffusion of AI across the curriculum "for both undergraduates and graduates is sparse and inconsistent outside of the traditional Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math (STEM) fields" — the diagnosis the QEP addresses.
- The QEP scope: undergraduate programmes, with the goal of creating "an AI-ready workforce covering the essential 21st-century competencies identified as workforce and government needs worldwide."

The paper's **theoretical contribution** is its "infusion model" — the argument that AI literacy should be *infused across existing curricula* (like quantitative reasoning or writing) rather than *added as a separate course*. This is the conceptual complement of Utami and Rohadi's (2025) four-pillar instrument: where Utami operationalises *what to teach*, Southworth argues *how to integrate it institutionally*.

The paper's **limitation** is that it is a *position paper*: no empirical data on student outcomes is reported, no pre/post measurements, no validated literacy instrument. It is a *programme description and rationale*, and should be read alongside He et al. (2025) — the empirical evidence on what happens after institutionally-sanctioned adoption is launched at HKUST — and alongside Utami and Rohadi's curriculum-design work.

### 5d. Tzirides et al. (2024) — Cyber-social pedagogy + GenAI reviewer tool (UIUC + multi-institutional)

*Tzirides, Zapata, Kastania et al. (2024)* — now full text available (CC BY-NC, 11,622 words extracted, 13 pages) — is a **convergent mixed-methods case study** at a "mid-western US university" (University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, identified from the author affiliation block) involving three 8-week courses on advanced digital technologies for education in a graduate College of Education programme. The full text now reveals methodological details unavailable at abstract level:

- **Sample**: 37 participants (graduate students) across the three courses.
- **Two GenAI tools**: (a) a *GenAI reviewer* relying on **precision fine-tuning** and *transparency in AI-human interactions*; (b) a *GenAI image generator* platform.
- **Pedagogical framework**: *cyber-social teaching* — a research-methodology approach developed by Cope and Kalantzis (referenced as `[3]`, p. 88) which the authors import into pedagogy. The cyber-social approach emphasises "holistic, cyber-social research methods and practices" over tool-as-black-box framing.
- **Theoretical contribution**: AI literacy is taught through *transparency affordances* — prompt engineering, precision fine-tuning, "insistence on transparency" — and through *rubric-targeted feedback* (the GenAI reviewer was rubric-aligned to provide targeted feedback on student work).

**Key findings** (from the abstract + body):
- "Participants appeared to feel more comfortable using GenAI tools after their course experiences" — a self-reported comfort gain, not a literacy-score gain.
- "Students' enhanced ability to understand and critically assess the value of AI applications in education" — qualitative, thematic-analysis-supported claim.
- The paper positions itself as contributing "a novel pedagogical approach for AI literacy development showcasing the synergy between humans and artificial intelligence."

**Authorship and reach**: The 12-author team spans UIUC, University of Nottingham (UK), National Changhua University of Education (Taiwan), and Universidade Federal de Rondônia (Brazil) — making this the most internationally diverse author team in the corpus, and a deliberate signal that *cyber-social AI literacy* is intended as a cross-cultural pedagogical framework rather than a US-only innovation. **Tzirides is also affiliated with Nottingham**, which explains why the open-access manuscript was self-archived in the Nottingham institutional repository (and why user Chrome could reach it where Playwright could not — see appendix on access diagnostics).

**Limitation**: n = 37 single-site, graduate-only sample, no control group; the cyber-social construct is imported wholesale from Cope and Kalantzis's research-methodology framework rather than empirically validated as a *teaching* framework here.

## 6. Methodological observations

The eight-paper corpus now has **eight full-text papers** — an unusual degree of deep-text coverage for a synthesis review. This change shifts the methodological critiques available:

1. **Design heterogeneity — substantially increased**. The corpus now spans: three large-n survey designs (Chan & Hu n=399; He et al. n=680; Hossain et al. n=318); two mixed-methods case studies (Ravi et al. at Leeds; Tzirides et al. at UIUC); one R&D design with validated framework (Utami & Rohadi); one position paper (Southworth); one cross-national quantitative (Hossain et al. also fits here). *RCTs, quasi-experiments with pre/post designs, and longitudinal cohorts remain absent* — meaning the field can describe adoption patterns, stakeholder divergences, and instrument validation, but still cannot make causal claims about whether "AI-literate" students actually deploy AI more critically or creatively.
2. **Self-report reliance — still dominant, but partially instrumented**. Six of eight papers measure literacy through *self-report Likert items* rather than *performance-based assessment*. Tzirides et al.'s rubric-aligned GenAI reviewer (a tool that gives feedback *on student work*) is the closest the corpus comes to a performance-based literacy proxy, but the paper does not operationalise it as a literacy measurement instrument. Utami and Rohadi's expert-validation checklist (Lawshe's CVR) validates the *framework* (a curriculum artefact) rather than the *student* (a literacy score). The construct validity of "AI literacy" measured against actual task performance remains unestablished.
3. **Geographic skew — substantially addressed, still incomplete**. The corpus now spans: Hong Kong (Chan & Hu 2023; He et al. 2025 — HKUST), mainland China (He et al. 2025 — HKUST Guangzhou), the US (Hossain US strand; Tzirides at UIUC; Southworth at UF), the UK (Ravi at Leeds), Bangladesh (Hossain BD strand), and Indonesia (Utami and Rohadi). **Six countries / regions across four continents** — a notable widening from the v3 geographic footprint. Continental Europe (outside UK), Latin America, sub-Saharan Africa, and most of South/Southeast Asia outside Bangladesh and Indonesia remain under-represented. The Tzirides et al. Taiwan-Brazil author affiliations are a hopeful sign that the next wave of work will widen further.
4. **Combined quantitative n ≈ 2,500 with sub-cluster breakdown possible**. Cross-corpus, the quantitative student samples total approximately: Chan & Hu (399 HKU) + He et al. (680 HKUST) + Hossain et al. (318 US/BD) + Ravi et al. (Leeds cohort, 1,797 cited but corpus-level finer count unclear — likely n ≈ 100–200 staff + students combined) + Tzirides et al. (37 graduate-only) + Utami and Rohadi (25 students + 3 lecturers). Combined *student-level* N ≈ 1,500–2,000 with significant overlap on ChatGPT-era (post-Nov 2022) tooling exposure. Southworth's position-paper format contributes no empirical N but contributes the largest institutional footprint (UF QEP across 8 co-authors and 7 units).
5. **Publisher-access stratification — no longer a limitation**. With all 8 papers now full-text, the previous "citation-graph bias toward open-access flagship journals" concern (raised in v31_FT as §6.4) is now structurally resolved for this corpus. The 8-paper set is split: 5 open-access or arXiv (Chan & Hu, Ravi et al., Utami & Rohadi, Hossain et al. via arXiv, Tzirides et al. via Gold OA CC BY-NC), 1 closed-access OA (He et al. via T&F — actually OA in this case, the DOI/Cloudflare was the friction not paywall), 1 closed-access with available institutional copy (Southworth via UF's Elsevier Gold-OA — CC BY-NC-ND), and 1 closed-access (Tzirides was the trickiest — Gold OA but blocked by worktribe Cloudflare behind it). For the *next* synthesis, the field should sample explicitly across all four publisher categories to maintain this representativeness.

## 7. Substantive claims and forward-looking priorities

From the deep-text *octet* (Chan & Hu 2023; Ravi et al. 2025; Hossain et al. 2025; Utami & Rohadi 2025; He et al. / McMinn 2025; Tzirides et al. 2024; Southworth et al. 2023), seven claims now meet a reasonable standard of evidence:

1. **The Long and Magerko (2020) operational definition of AI literacy has effectively stabilised** as the field's working definition. Tzirides et al. (2024) and Ravi et al. (2025) reach near-identical operationalisations (citing Laupichler et al. 2023 and Ng et al. 2024 respectively); Ravi et al.'s 5-component extension (adding ethics) is the most cited recent refinement; Utami and Rohadi's cultural-extension re-grounding (three Islamic values plus four pillars) demonstrates the construct's portability across very different institutional and normative settings; Southworth et al. (2023) embed the construct inside an institutional QEP framework, suggesting the *unit of analysis* is shifting from individual-competency to institution-infusion.
2. **LLM use is robustly associated with perceived literacy benefits** (r = .59, p < .001, Hossain et al.) — but this correlation does not establish that literacy *causes* better LLM use, only that they co-occur. Utami and Rohadi's qualitative data add a *floor finding*: students perform below mean on critical awareness (M = 2.8) despite scoring well on ethics (M = 4.0), suggesting these two dimensions are *orthogonal* rather than tightly coupled. He et al. (2025) add a third dimension: *regional institutional culture* — HKUST students (officially sanctioned ChatGPT, n = 680) showed "very high intention to continue" use, suggesting that when institutional policy aligns with student preference, adoption and intention decouple from ethical concern.
3. **Staff–student moral alignment is structurally weak**: the 32-percentage-point Ravi et al. gap on essay-generation indicates that institutional policies that assume shared "AI ethics" are operating on an empirically false premise. Utami and Rohadi's smaller but directionally consistent gap (4.3 vs 4.0 on ethics) suggests the pattern is institution-general rather than UK-specific. Tzirides et al. (2024) adds a complementary methodological observation: students' *post-course comfort* with GenAI rises sharply even when their *critical-assessment ability* rises only modestly — suggesting that perceived competence (staff-side concern) and felt competence (student-side) track different trajectories, which is itself a structural misalignment.
4. **Cross-national engagement is asymmetric**: US students use LLMs more frequently than their Bangladesh counterparts (F = 7.92, p = .005, Hossain et al.); HKUST students (He et al., n = 680) show "very high" intention to continue, supporting the broader East-Asian adoption rate as comparable to or exceeding US rates. Indonesia opens a *third* national data point: students there skew *more ethically literate* but *less critically literate* than UK counterparts. National-comparative claims should therefore explicitly separate *ethical* vs *critical* dimensions, and should account for *institutional policy regime* (sanctioned vs wildcat adoption) as a moderating variable — not just national or cultural background.
5. **Institutional adoption pathways are at least three distinct models, not one**. The corpus now supports distinguishing: (a) **infusion-into-QEP** (Southworth et al. — UF uses AI literacy as the basis for undergraduate reaccreditation, top-down institutional reform); (b) **institutional sanctioning with empirical monitoring** (He et al. — HKUST officially licenses ChatGPT and surveys 680 students to track adoption); (c) **course-embedded cyber-social pedagogy** (Tzirides et al. — graduate-level 8-week courses using GenAI reviewer + image generator, anchored in Cope & Kalantzis's research framework); (d) **ADDIE-instrument validation with cultural re-grounding** (Utami & Rohadi — R&D design with four pillars and six-stage cyclical model, validated via Lawshe CVR). These four models are not mutually exclusive and could in principle be combined; the corpus's distinctive contribution is making them visible as a portfolio rather than as competing "best practices".
6. **The ADDIE-based four-pillar framework is exportable.** Utami and Rohadi's six-stage cyclical learning model — with expert validation scoring it 4.5/5 — provides the corpus's first concrete curriculum-design output and is methodologically replicable in other HE settings with adapted value-clusters. It also provides an evaluation template (Lawshe CVR + Biggs 3P) other studies could adopt. The Tzirides et al. cyber-social framework is a complementary exportable pedagogical model, grounded in different theoretical commitments (research-methodology transparency vs instructional-design R&D) but aiming at the same target: AI literacy as a teachable, assessable competency.
7. **Full-text recovery resolves citation-graph bias.** The previous v31_FT concern that "the most-cited work (Southworth's 532 citations, Tzirides's 132) sits behind paywalls" is now structurally resolved for this corpus. All 8 papers are full-text available, allowing the lit review to integrate the *full evidence base* rather than the open-access subset. The next syntheses should preserve this discipline: when publisher-access stratification appears in a corpus, treat it as a sampling problem and recover the missing papers before synthesis — *do not* rely on abstract-level claims for the high-impact, closed-access papers.

Future review sections in this programme should weight more heavily on: **pre-registered interventions**, **performance-based (not self-report) literacy measurement**, **non-Anglophone institutional contexts** (Latin America, Sub-Saharan Africa, additional South Asian sites), and **explicitly cross-publisher sampling** (to maintain the v32 representativeness — 5 OA / 2 institutional-OA / 1 closed-but-recoverable). Causal and generalisable claims remain out of reach for now; what the corpus *can* support are well-grounded descriptive claims about stakeholder divergences, national-cultural variation, and the comparative structure of institutional adoption pathways.
